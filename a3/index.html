Gender of
Electricity

What uneven burdens do Nigerian women face when using dirty fuels in the home?

Under what types of conditions are preferences for cleaner options expressed?

This series highlights gender disparities in a head of household's (hh) fuel choices through a handful of lenses. The main factors considered are education, use case, and accessability of infrastructure. World Bank household data offers a variety of notable contrasts from these perspectives. A selection of the largest differences are visualized here.


A possible reason for why these disparities exist will be explored via data from the Institute for Health Metrics and their Global Burden of Disease database.


Solid fuel and kerosene use for lighting and cooking is common across Nigeria. As a result the nation's poor and underserved communities have an increased likelihood of many respiratory diseases. As women are still responsible for the majority of cooking across the country, they and their children are subjected to a greater burden of such diseases.


A better understanding may be possible of women's choice for cleaner, and safer fuel options by factoring in their health, education, and access to public infrastructure.


The UNDP has a unique opprotunity among these affected individuals to grow a community that will both support their energy goals and, once and for all, address the gender delineated inequities that are the hallmark of solid fuel and kerosene use.
