Gender of
Electricity

A household head's preference for lighting and cooking fuel has the potential to inform us about their receptiveness to renewables and the conditions under which such receptivity may manifest.


This series highlights disparities in fuel decisions by gender and through a handful of lenses. The main factors considered are education, use case, and accessability of infrastructure. World Bank household data offers a variety of notable contrasts from these perspectives. A selection of the largest differences are visualized here.


A possible reason for why these disparities exist will be explored via data from the Institute for Health Metrics and their Global Burden of Disease database.


Solid fuel and kerosene use for lighting and cooking is common across Nigeria. As a result the nation's poor and underserved communities have an increased likelihood of a variety of respiratory diseases. As women are still responsible for the majority of cooking across the country, they and their children are subjected to a greater burden of such diseases.


It is possible that we can understand women's choice for cleaner, and safer fuel options by factoring in their health, education, and access to public infrastructure.


The UNDP has a unique opprotunity among these affected individuals to grow a community that will support renewable energy goals and, once and for all, address the gender delineated inequities that are the hallmark of solid fuel and kerosene use.
