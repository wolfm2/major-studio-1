A household head's preference for lighting and cooking fuel can speak to their receptiveness to renewables, and lived experience vis-Ã -vis gender.


This series investigates gendered head of household fuel decisions through a handful of lenses. The main factors considered are education, use case, and accessability of infrastructure. World Bank household data offers a variety of interesting stories from these perspectives. A few of the largest disparities are highlighted here.


Why these disparities exist will be explored via data from the Institute for Health Metrics.


Lastly, female representation in salient renewable infrastructure decision making bodies will be contrasted against the impact such decisions have on female health.

Solid fuel and kerosene use for lighting and cooking is common across Nigeria. As a result the nation's poor and underserved communities have an increased likelihood of a variety of respiratory diseases. As women are still responsible for the majority of cooking across the country, they and their children are subjected to a greater burden of such diseases.


From the last section it is clear that this inequity is, in some capacity, understood and reflected in women's choice for cleaner, and safer options for lighting and cooking.


The UNDP has a unique opprotunity among these affected individuals to grow a community that will support renewable energy goals and, once and for all, address the gender delineated inequities that are the hallmark of solid fuel and kerosene use.
